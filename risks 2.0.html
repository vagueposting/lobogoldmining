The use of mercury in the mining industry to amalgamate precious metals dates from about 2700 B.C.E (Lacerda 1997a; Rojas et al 2001). The total number of gold miners around the world using mercury to produce gold ranges from 3 to 5 million including 650,000 in Brazil, 250,000 in Tasmania, 250,000 in Indonesia and 150,000 in Vietnam (Jernelov and Ramel 1994). In modern Brazil, where gold rush has been present since 1980, at least 2000 tons of mercury were released, contaminating sediments, soils, fish, and human tissues; a similar situation happened in Colombia, Venezuela, Peru, and Bolivia (Malm 1998). Cases of human mercury contamination have been reported from various sites around the world ever since it was used as the major technique to produce gold and other precious metals in South America hundreds of years ago (de Lacerda and Solomons 1998). Mercury poisoning in humans is due to the elevated mercury concentrations in air, water, diet, hair, urine, blood, and other tissues (Eisler 2003). The presence of mercury in living organisms is one of the causes of cancer, birth defects, and other undesirable outcomes (Eisler 2000). In the case of Lobo, cyanide spill is the main threat.
